# Flagship Treatment Visual Directions

> **Emotional and Cinematic Identity for Cosmo's Core Treatment Ecosystem**
>
> This document defines the visual architecture for each flagship treatment — ensuring every image, crop, overlay, and before/after comparison feels emotionally distinct while remaining visually unified within the Cosmo universe.

**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** Active  
**Scope:** Microneedling · HydraFacial · Chemical Peels  
**Parent Document:** `docs/cosmo-visual-language.md`

---

## Core Philosophy

This document exists to prevent **treatment-page visual sameness** while preserving **ecosystem-level consistency**.

Each flagship treatment must feel:
- **Emotionally distinct** — you should *feel* the difference between a Microneedling page and a HydraFacial page before reading a single word
- **Visually related** — all three pages must clearly belong to the same Cosmo universe, sharing the same deep-navy foundation, gold accents, and editorial restraint

The site must **never** feel like random stock imagery stitched together.

## 1. MICRONEEDLING

### Emotional Tone

Precise. Procedural. Textural. Controlled. Sophisticated. Clinically luxurious.

The imagery should communicate **quiet technological sophistication** — the sense that what is happening is medically advanced yet deeply intentional. This is not aggressive cosmetic marketing; it is the visual equivalent of a skilled artisan working with precision instruments.

### Visual Atmosphere

The Microneedling treatment space should read as a **controlled environment of precision**. Warm ambient light, cool navy shadows, and subtle gold highlights create depth without drama. The room feels calm, ordered, and clinical-but-warm — like a高端 laboratory where craftsmanship meets medicine.

**Mood keywords:** Controlled · Precise · Serene · Technical · Intimate · Authoritative

### Lighting Philosophy

- **Focused directional contrast** — light falls on the treatment area (face/neck) with deliberate intent
- **Cooler navy shadows** — shadow areas lean toward navy/indigo rather than pure black, maintaining the Cosmo palette
- **Subtle warm highlights** — edge lighting on the practitioner's hands and the device creates separation without glare
- **Avoid:** flat procedural lighting, overhead fluorescent, or ring-light bounce
- **Reference:** Editorial medical photography with chiaroscuro influence — think *Annie Leibovitz portraits of surgeons*, not *clinical documentation*

### Composition Strategy

- **Close-up editorial detail** — tight frames on the needle cartridge approaching skin, practitioner's gloved hands in focus
- **Partial procedural framing** — show only the treatment area and the practitioner's hands; the patient's face is suggested, not centered
- **Asymmetrical focus** — the needle device occupies one third of the frame, the skin occupies another third, the remaining third is negative space with warm ambient light
- **Macro texture moments** — extreme close-ups of skin texture before treatment (showing pores, fine lines, scarring) as context for the transformative process
- **Avoid:** Full-face patient portraits during treatment; centered device shots; wide room shots

### Treatment Storytelling Style

The narrative arc for Microneedling imagery:
1. **Preparation** — Cleansed skin, numbing cream applied, equipment laid out with precision (tray composition)
2. **The Procedure** — Micro-needle device in motion, skin reacting with mild erythema (controlled redness, not alarming)
3. **Immediate After** — Soothing serum applied, skin calm but awake, subtle pink tone
4. **Healing Progression** — Day 1 (mild redness), Day 3 (normalized), Day 7 (texture improvement visible)

Storytelling should feel like a **documentary of precision** — each image is a single frame from a procedural film where every tool has purpose.

### Environmental Mood

The treatment room should feel **intimate and focused** — the outside world does not exist. The frame contains only:
- The treatment chair (partial — armrest, headrest suggesting patient presence)
- The Microneedling device (visible but not dominant — it is a tool, not the subject)
- Warm ambient light sources (diffused, indirect)
- Dark navy walls fading into shadow
- A single gold accent (a tray edge, a lamp base, a product bottle)

The environment should **never** feel like a white-walled medical office or a spa treatment room.

### Texture Language

Texture is the **primary visual vocabulary** for Microneedling:
- Skin texture before treatment (pores, acne scars, fine lines — honest, not exaggerated)
- The micro-needle cartridge (metallic, precise, sterile)
- Gloved hands (latex texture, clinical elegance)
- Serum droplets on skin (reflective, liquid, healing)
- Post-treatment skin (subtle pink, tightened pores, natural luminosity)

Texture should be **visible, not smoothed away**. This is not an airbrushed treatment — it is a textural transformation.

### Color Emphasis

- **Primary palette:** Deep navy (`#0d0d1a`), warm off-white (`#f9f6f1`), muted gold (`#c9a96e`)
- **Lighting accent:** Warm gold edge light (60-70% saturation, never brassy)
- **Shadow tone:** Navy-indigo blend (`#0d0d1a` to `#1a1a2e`)
- **Skin tone:** Honest skin — natural undertones, visible texture, mild procedural erythema (subtle pink)
- **Avoid:** Cool blue procedural light, orange-heavy skin tones, desaturated medical white

### Human Presence Rules

- **Practitioner's hands** are the primary human element — gloved, focused, in motion
- **Patient** appears only as partial presence: profile silhouette, neck/chin area, hand gripping armrest
- **No full patient faces** during treatment — the procedure is the subject, not the person
- **No eye contact** — the patient's eyes are closed or outside the frame
- **Dr. Rivera** may appear in the treatment context with warm, focused expression — not smiling, but present and attentive

### Mobile Crop Priorities

**Order of focal preservation on mobile (375-430px viewport):**
1. Needle device touching skin (primary action)
2. Practitioner's hands (context and trust)
3. Skin texture detail (the transformation surface)
4. Ambient treatment room (atmospheric context)

**Specific mobile guidelines:**
- Hero crop: Center on the treatment interaction zone (device + skin). Top third may show practitioner's forearms.
- Overview collage images: Each cell must have a clear single focal point — no busy compositions at mobile scale.
- Procedure steps: Keep the device and hands fully visible. Crop environmental context as needed.
- Before/after: Use vertical split or stacked layout for comparison. Never crop the comparison area.

### Approved Imagery Examples (Conceptual)

- Macro close-up of microneedling cartridge approaching skin with warm rim light
- Practitioner's gloved hands preparing the device on a dark-navy tray with gold edge
- Side-profile partial frame: patient's jawline and neck with device in contact, warm focus light
- Before texture close-up: visible pores and acne scars under controlled directional light
- Post-treatment skin: subtle erythema, tightened texture, natural luminosity, no filters

### Forbidden Visual Directions

- Blood drops or streaks on skin (even controlled bleeding reads as alarming)
- Needle penetration close-ups that emphasize pain or invasion
- Dramatic erythema (angry red skin) as a visual statement
- Full-face patient with device applied (reads as medical torture)
- Sterile white treatment rooms with fluorescent lighting
- Aggressive before/after comparisons with extreme lighting differences
- Smiling patient immediately after treatment (reads as inauthentic)

### Overlay Behavior

- **Hero overlay:** Left-to-right gradient (`rgba(13,13,26,0.82)` at text zone → transparent at device zone)
- **Mobile overlay:** Top-to-bottom gradient to preserve the device-skin interaction zone
- **No full-image overlays** — the treatment detail must remain fully visible at all breakpoints
- **Text placement:** Text sits in the left third (or top on mobile), leaving the right (or bottom) two-thirds for the unfiltered treatment interaction
- **Opacity ceiling:** Max `0.82` — higher opacity crushes the procedural detail

### Before/After Philosophy

**Subtle refinement, not miracle transformation.**

Microneedling before/after comparisons should communicate:
- Skin texture improvement (pore size reduction, scar softening)
- Overall skin quality elevation (radiance, evenness)
- Progressive collagen remodeling over 3-6 months

**Specific guidelines:**
- Same lighting, same angle, same expression in before and after
- Visible skin texture in both images (no blurring or softening the "before")
- Realistic timeline: 3-session series, not single-session miracles
- Caption language: "Texture improved after 3 sessions" not "Dramatic transformation"
- Side-by-side or slider format only — no stacked comparisons that exaggerate difference through scale
- Before and after should feel like **the same skin, improved** — not two different people

## 2. HYDRAFACIAL

### Emotional Tone

Calm. Hydrated. Fluid. Glowing. Restorative. Soft luxury.

The imagery should communicate **spa-meets-clinical sophistication** — a treatment that is both medically effective and deeply relaxing. This is the most approachable of the three flagship treatments, visually welcoming without sacrificing clinical credibility.

### Visual Atmosphere

The HydraFacial experience should read as **luminous and serene**. Soft light, fluid reflections, and gentle motion create an atmosphere of pampering that still feels medically credible. The treatment chair is a place of rest, not procedure.

**Mood keywords:** Calm · Restorative · Luminous · Soft · Fresh · Serene · Welcoming

### Lighting Philosophy

- **Soft warm diffusion** — the dominant light source is diffused through fabric or frosted glass, creating a gentle glow
- **Gentle edge lighting** — subtle rim light on the patient's profile and the HydraFacial handpiece, creating dimensional separation
- **Less contrast than Microneedling** — shadows are softer, highlights are gentler, the overall tonal range is compressed
- **Avoid:** Directional contrast that feels clinical or interrogative
- **Reference:** Editorial beauty photography for luxury skincare — think *Paolo Roversi softness*, but with medical credibility

### Composition Strategy

- **Negative space as a feature** — generous breathing room around the treatment creates a sense of calm and spaciousness
- **Fluidity** — serums, water, and glow create organic visual flow across the frame
- **Environmental calm** — the treatment chair, lighting fixture, and room architecture are part of the composition
- **Partial patient presence** — closed eyes, relaxed profile, peaceful expression (eyes closed, never looking at camera)
- **Device integration** — the HydraFacial handpiece is elegant and visible but not dominant; it should look like a precision tool, not a medical instrument
- **Avoid:** Tight macro shots of the suction process; crowded frames with multiple visual elements

### Treatment Storytelling Style

The narrative arc for HydraFacial imagery:
1. **Preparation** — Clean, bare skin; the canvas is ready. Gentle lighting on a calm face.
2. **The Flow** — The vortex handpiece gliding across skin with visible serum. Soft reflections on the skin surface.
3. **Extraction Moment** — Gentle suction visible but not sensationalized. The focus is on the glow, not the debris.
4. **The Reveal** — Post-treatment radiance. The skin is luminous, hydrated, fresh. The patient looks peacefully renewed.

Storytelling should feel like **a visual meditation on skincare** — each image is a chapter in a story about renewal, not a technical manual.

### Environmental Mood

The HydraFacial room should feel **like a luxury sanctuary**:
- Soft ambient light from indirect sources (wall sconces, backlit panels)
- The treatment chair positioned to catch the best light — not centered but angled for visual interest
- Minimal visible equipment — the HydraFacial device should be elegant enough to be part of the decor
- Warm navy walls with subtle gold reflections
- Fresh flowers, soft textiles, or other spa-level details (but restrained — not Instagram-cluttered)

The environment should feel **luxurious but not opulent** — premium materials, calm atmosphere, nothing performative.

### Texture Language

HydraFacial's texture language is about **fluidity and smoothness**:
- Glowing skin surface with visible hydration (dewy, not greasy)
- Serum droplets and fluid movement across the skin
- The vortex handpiece (smooth, contoured, elegant)
- Clean, bare skin with natural texture — never poreless or plastic
- Soft fabric of the treatment chair or draping

Texture should feel **fresh and alive** — skin that breathes and reflects light naturally, not a masked or filtered surface.

### Color Emphasis

- **Primary palette:** Deep navy (`#0d0d1a`), warm off-white (`#f9f6f1`), muted gold (`#c9a96e`)
- **Lighting accent:** Warm golden diffusion — slightly warmer than Microneedling's edge lighting
- **Skin tone:** Healthy, hydrated, naturally radiant — visible life in the skin
- **Fluid accent:** The serums can catch gold light, creating subtle amber reflections
- **Avoid:** Cool white clinical tones, desaturated "medical" skin, green-tinged fluid

### Human Presence Rules

- **Patient** is more visible here than in Microneedling — closed eyes, relaxed expression, peaceful demeanor
- **Full profile or three-quarter view** is acceptable — the patient is serene, not posing
- **Eyes must be closed** — open eyes break the meditative mood
- **Practitioner's hands** are gentle, holding the handpiece with care — firm but soft
- **No influencer expressions** — no smiling, no "omg this feels amazing" faces
- **Dr. Rivera** can appear in gentle consultation context or performing the treatment with warm, attentive presence

### Mobile Crop Priorities

**Order of focal preservation on mobile (375-430px viewport):**
1. Patient's face/profile with glowing skin (primary emotional anchor)
2. HydraFacial handpiece and serum flow (treatment context)
3. Soft environmental lighting (atmospheric context)
4. Treatment room luxury details (secondary)

**Specific mobile guidelines:**
- Hero crop: Center on the patient's relaxed profile with the handpiece visible. The glow should be unmistakable.
- Overview collage images: Soft, serene compositions that look good even at small scale.
- Procedure steps: Show the handpiece-skin interaction clearly. Maintain the sense of gentle motion.
- Before/after: Side-by-side preferred; if stacked, the "after" must lead (shows first).

### Approved Imagery Examples (Conceptual)

- Patient in three-quarter profile with closed eyes, warm diffused light on glowing skin, HydraFacial handpiece gently touching the cheek
- Top-down angle: patient lying back, practitioner leaning in with the handpiece, soft lighting creating a halo effect
- Close-up of serum glistening on skin with the handpiece in frame, gold reflections in the fluid
- Post-treatment portrait: patient with fresh, glowing skin, natural expression, peaceful eyes
- Environmental shot: empty treatment chair with soft lighting, elegant device, warm atmosphere — inviting and calm

### Forbidden Visual Directions

- Extreme close-ups of debris extraction (pore plugs, oil, dirt — sensationalized)
- Tight macro shots of the suction tip on skin (reads as aggressive)
- Influencer-style "glow up" selfies with exaggerated expressions
- Overly glossy, high-contrast skin shine (looks greasy, not hydrated)
- Brassy gold color correction that overwhelms the natural skin tone
- Cold blue or green procedural lighting
- "Before" images that exaggerate poor skin quality to make results seem more dramatic

### Overlay Behavior

- **Hero overlay:** Left-to-right gradient, slightly lighter than Microneedling (use `rgba(13,13,26,0.75)` to preserve the luminous mood)
- **Mobile overlay:** Top-to-bottom gradient — the glow of the skin must remain visible at the bottom of the frame
- **Lighter touch overall** — HydraFacial's hero should feel more transparent, more luminous, less opaque
- **Text placement:** Text in the upper-left (or top on mobile), leaving the patient's skin and the handpiece fully visible
- **Opacity ceiling:** Max `0.75` — the luminosity of the treatment must shine through

### Before/After Philosophy

**Freshness and vitality, not extreme transformation.**

HydraFacial before/after comparisons should communicate:
- Immediate glow and radiance
- Improved skin hydration (visible plumpness, reduced fine dehydration lines)
- Cleaner pores and more even texture
- Overall skin health and vitality

**Specific guidelines:**
- Same lighting, same angle, same expression in both images
- The "after" should look like **the same person after a great night's sleep** — not a different person after heavy makeup
- Immediate results are the key selling point — the before/after gap should be visible but natural
- Caption language: "Immediate glow after one session" not "Unrecognizable transformation"
- Avoid dramatizing the comparison through different lighting or makeup
- The "after" skin should look **hydrated and alive** — not powdered, filtered, or retouched

## 3. CHEMICAL PEELS

### Emotional Tone

Renewal. Refinement. Resurfacing. Controlled transformation. Elegant restoration.

The imagery should communicate **editorial skin renewal** — the idea that the skin is being carefully, intentionally resurfaced to reveal what lies beneath. This treatment sits between Microneedling's precision and HydraFacial's softness in visual intensity.

### Visual Atmosphere

The Chemical Peel experience should feel **like a controlled studio environment** — clinical enough to inspire confidence, elegant enough to feel luxurious. The focus is on the solution, the application, and the emerging skin beneath.

**Mood keywords:** Renewing · Transformative · Controlled · Refined · Clinical-elegant · Restorative

### Lighting Philosophy

- **Slightly more dramatic than HydraFacial** — there is a sense of transformation happening, and the lighting reflects this
- **Softer than Microneedling** — the drama comes from atmosphere, not from high-contrast procedural lighting
- **Warm ambient base with subtle directional accent** — a wash of warm light over the treatment area with a gentle key light on the practitioner's hands
- **Skin catchlight** — the wet surface of the peel solution creates subtle reflections that add visual interest
- **Avoid:** Harsh contrast, deep shadows, or anything that reads as aggressive
- **Reference:** Editorial fashion beauty meets medical precision — think *Peter Lindbergh's naturalism* with controlled clinical accents

### Composition Strategy

- **Environmental elegance** — the treatment room is visible and contributes to the atmosphere (unlike Microneedling's tight focus)
- **Surface texture focus** — the peel application on skin, the solution spreading, the skin's response
- **Refined asymmetry** — the practitioner applies solution from one side, leaving the other side of the frame open
- **Layered depth** — foreground (the applicator), midground (the skin), background (warm ambient room)
- **Clinical preparation context** — organized treatment tray, cotton pads, solutions in clean glass containers
- **Avoid:** Tight macro shots of peeling/flaking skin; centered, symmetrical procedural compositions

### Treatment Storytelling Style

The narrative arc for Chemical Peel imagery:
1. **Consultation & Assessment** — Skin analysis, peel selection discussion, professional attention
2. **Preparation** — Clean skin, degreasing, eye protection applied — the canvas is prepped
3. **Application** — The solution is applied with precision. The skin's response is monitored. Controlled, calm, professional.
4. **Neutralization** — The moment of transformation. The solution is neutralized, and the fresh skin begins to emerge.
5. **Healing & Reveal** — Peeling process (day 2-5 shown with restraint), fresh skin revealed (day 7+)

Storytelling should feel **like a dermatological documentary** — each phase is documented with clinical honesty and editorial elegance.

### Environmental Mood

The Chemical Peel room should feel **refined and professional**:
- Organized treatment surfaces — clean lines, purposeful arrangement of materials
- The treatment chair positioned for clinical access and good lighting
- Visible but elegant equipment — the peel solutions should look like medical-grade products (clean glass, professional labels)
- Warm navy walls with gold undertones in the lighting
- Minimal visual distraction — the treatment is the focus

This environment is **more clinical than HydraFacial, less intimate than Microneedling** — it occupies a deliberate middle ground.

### Texture Language

Chemical Peel's texture language is about **transformation through the skin's surface**:
- Peel solution on skin (liquid, reflective, active)
- Skin texture before (rough, photodamaged, uneven tone)
- The peeling process (fine flakes, not dramatic sheets — shown with restraint)
- Fresh skin revealed (smooth, refined, even-toned)
- The applicator and preparation tools (clean, precise, medical-grade)

Texture should communicate **controlled change** — the skin is transitioning from one state to another under professional supervision.

### Color Emphasis

- **Primary palette:** Deep navy (`#0d0d1a`), warm off-white (`#f9f6f1`), muted gold (`#c9a96e`)
- **Solution accent:** The peel solution itself — slightly tinged (yellowish for glycolic, clear for other acids)
- **Skin response:** Subtle pink/warmth indicating the peel is active — controlled, not alarming
- **Fresh skin:** Even, refined tone with natural warmth — the "after" should look restored
- **Avoid:** Reddened or irritated skin tones; heavily desaturated medical white; orange skin from poor color correction

### Human Presence Rules

- **Practitioner's hands** are primary — gloved, precise, applying solution with controlled movements
- **Patient** is partially visible — the treatment area (face/neck) is shown, but the full face is not the subject
- **Eye protection** is visible — the goggles add a clinical touch that reinforces medical credibility
- **Patient's eyes** may be visible during pre-treatment consultation but not during application
- **No expressions of discomfort** — the patient should appear calm and relaxed throughout
- **Dr. Rivera** can appear in the consultation context (warm, attentive, discussing options) or application context (focused, precise)

### Mobile Crop Priorities

**Order of focal preservation on mobile (375-430px viewport):**
1. Peel solution application zone (primary action)
2. Practitioner's gloved hands with applicator (precision context)
3. Skin surface texture (the transformation surface)
4. Environmental treatment room (professional context)

**Specific mobile guidelines:**
- Hero crop: Focus on the application area — the solution on skin, the practitioner's hands. Maintain the sense of controlled precision.
- Overview collage images: Clean clinical compositions that translate well at small scale.
- Procedure steps: Show solution + skin + hands clearly. Avoid empty space that doesn't add value.
- Before/after: Use vertical split. The post-peel skin should be the hero on mobile.

### Approved Imagery Examples (Conceptual)

- Top-down composition: treatment tray with organized solutions, cotton pads, applicator — arranged with precision
- Practitioner applying solution with cotton pad to patient's cheek, warm focus light on the application zone
- Side-profile: patient with eye protection, solution visible on skin, practitioner's hands in frame
- Day 3 post-peel: fine flaking texture (shown with restraint — editorial, not alarming)
- Day 7 reveal: fresh, refined skin with even tone and natural radiance

### Forbidden Visual Directions

- Close-ups of skin peeling in large sheets (looks like sunburn, not luxury)
- Red, irritated skin emphasized for dramatic effect
- "Before" images that exaggerate sun damage or hyperpigmentation
- Patient mid-peel with visible discomfort expressions
- Blood or broken skin from aggressive peeling
- Sterile white clinic environments with fluorescent lighting
- Overly dramatic before/after comparisons with different lighting conditions
- Harsh yellow or orange color casts that make the solution look unnatural

### Overlay Behavior

- **Hero overlay:** Left-to-right gradient — use `rgba(13,13,26,0.78)` (between Microneedling's 0.82 and HydraFacial's 0.75)
- **Mobile overlay:** Top-to-bottom gradient — the application zone must remain visible
- **Moderate opacity** — enough for text readability without hiding the clinical context
- **Text placement:** Upper-left (or top on mobile), leaving the application area and skin response visible
- **Opacity ceiling:** Max `0.78` — the peel application detail must be legible

### Before/After Philosophy

**Gradual refinement, luxury skincare evolution.**

Chemical Peel before/after comparisons should communicate:
- Improved skin texture and smoothness
- More even skin tone (reduced hyperpigmentation, sun damage fading)
- Refined pore appearance
- Overall skin quality elevation

**Specific guidelines:**
- Same lighting, same angle, same expression in both images
- The "after" should be **one peel series forward, not a complete identity change**
- Multiple session progression is more honest than single-session extremes
- Caption language: "Texture and tone improved after 3 sessions" not "Peeled away years of damage"
- The "before" should show real, untreated skin — not artificially worsened skin
- The "after" should show real, improved skin — not retouched or filtered perfection
- Always include a timeline reference: "Results shown after one series of peels" or "After 3 sessions over 8 weeks"

## 4. SHARED ECOSYSTEM CONSISTENCY RULES

### What ALL Treatments Must Share

Despite their emotional differences, every treatment page in the Cosmo ecosystem must share these visual DNA markers:

#### 1. Deep-Navy Cinematic Atmosphere
- Every hero background, every environmental shot, every detail frame must rest on the Cosmo navy foundation (`#0d0d1a` and `#1a1a2e`)
- No white backgrounds, no light gray rooms, no beige clinic walls
- The atmosphere is always **immersive** — the dark background creates the luxury container for all visual content

#### 2. Editorial Restraint
- Every image must feel curated, not captured
- Before any image enters the ecosystem, pass it through the editorial filter: "Would this appear in a luxury magazine?"
- If the answer is no, the image is not Cosmo

#### 3. Luxury Procedural Calm
- No image should communicate urgency, aggression, or medical panic
- Every treatment looks like it is being performed with care, precision, and time
- The patient is always calm; the practitioner is always focused; the environment is always controlled

#### 4. Gold as Editorial Accent, Not Decoration
- Gold appears as edge lighting, subtle reflections, and accent elements — never as a dominant color
- Gold should feel like **aged precious metal catching light**, not a design choice
- The gold in imagery should match the gold in CSS (`#c9a96e`): warm, muted, 60-70% saturation

#### 5. Asymmetrical Composition
- No centered subjects, no symmetrical framing, no stock-photo balance
- Every image should have a clear focal point that sits off-center
- The remaining space is negative breathing room — never filler

#### 6. Subtle Overlays
- All text overlays use the Cosmo gradient system (`rgba(13,13,26,0.75-0.82)`)
- Overlays never cover the primary visual anchor (treatment interaction zone)
- On mobile, the gradient shifts from horizontal to vertical but preserves the same philosophy

#### 7. Negative-Space Respect
- Text sits in negative space — never over busy image areas
- On mobile, hero text and CTAs must have clean backgrounds or full-width overlays
- If an image lacks clear negative space, it requires a stronger overlay on mobile

#### 8. Homepage Compatibility
- **The Homepage Test:** Every treatment image must feel visually cohesive if placed beside the homepage hero
- Same lighting temperature, same color palette, same compositional restraint
- If an image would look out of place next to the homepage hero (`assets/images/home-page.png`), it does not belong

#### 9. Consistent Color Temperature
- All treatment imagery must share the same warm color temperature
- Shadows lean navy, highlights lean warm gold, skin tones are natural
- No treatment page should have a different white balance than another
- The "feel" of the light should be identical across all treatment imagery

#### 10. No Makeup / Filter / Retouch
- Treatment imagery shows real skin — pores, texture, natural variation
- No airbrushing, no smoothing filters, no AI skin perfection
- The luxury comes from the **honest presentation of improvement**, not from hiding flaws

## 5. STRICT "DO NOT" RULES

### Non-Negotiable Visual Guardrails

These apply to **all treatment imagery** across the Cosmo ecosystem. Any image that violates these rules must be rejected or regenerated.

| ❌ Forbidden | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| **Neon beauty aesthetics** — high-saturation pastels, bright pinks, influencer-grade filters | Destroys the editorial luxury atmosphere; reads as beauty blogger, not medical premium |
| **Influencer beauty ads** — smiling patient holding products, mirror selfies, "glow up" before/afters | Reads as performative and inauthentic; Cosmo is not an influencer brand |
| **White corporate clinic stock imagery** — bright white walls, fluorescent ceiling panels, beige reception desks | Contradicts the deep-navy luxury atmosphere entirely; reads as cheap clinic template |
| **Overexposed procedural rooms** — blown-out windows, harsh overhead lighting, no atmosphere | Eliminates the cinematic quality that defines the Cosmo brand |
| **Glossy AI skin** — poreless, plastic, hyper-smooth renderings with no natural texture | Reads as fake; destroys medical credibility and trust in results |
| **Futuristic hologram aesthetics** — glowing blue UI overlays, AR interfaces, holographic patient data | Contradicts the restrained editorial tone; feels startup-tech, not luxury medical |
| **Cluttered medical equipment** — multiple devices, tangled wires, busy clinical backgrounds | Destroys the calm hierarchy; reads as chaotic and undermines the luxury atmosphere |
| **Exaggerated beauty retouching** — smoothed wrinkles, removed pores, altered skin texture | Destroys credibility; the "after" results must look achievable and real |
| **Hyper-saturated gold/orange** — brassy golds, neon oranges, warm filters applied in post | Violates the Cosmo gold philosophy (muted, aged precious metal — not synthetic warmth) |
| **Different lighting temperatures across pages** — one treatment with warm light, another with cool light | The cardinal sin; every page must feel visually consistent with every other page |
| **Centered stock-photo compositions** — subject dead-center, equal headroom, symmetrical balance | Reads as template, not editorial; destroys the curated, intentional feel |
| **Random stock imagery** — models unrelated to the clinic context, generic medical stock photos | Instantly reads as template site; destroys trust and visual cohesion |

## Final Ecosystem Integrity Rule

> **"Each treatment must feel emotionally distinct, but visually related."**

Run every candidate image through the **triple-filter test**:

1. **Treatment-Specific Filter:** Does this image communicate the correct emotional tone for this specific treatment? (Microneedling = precision, HydraFacial = luminosity, Chemical Peels = renewal)

2. **Ecosystem Filter:** Would this image feel visually cohesive if placed beside another treatment's hero image? Do they share the same lighting temperature, color palette, and compositional restraint?

3. **Homepage Filter:** Could this image naturally belong on the homepage? Does it feel like it came from the same photo shoot as the homepage hero?

If any filter returns **no**, the image does not belong in the Cosmo visual ecosystem.
